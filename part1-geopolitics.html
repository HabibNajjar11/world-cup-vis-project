Part 1 — Geopolitics and the balance of power

How the global expansion of football has (and hasn't) shifted the tournament's center of gravity. Men's FIFA World Cup, 1930–2022, 22 editions.

Viz 1 — The rise of the confederations

Share of all participating teams from each confederation, at every World Cup since 1930. A widening band means that confederation is claiming a bigger slice of the tournament; a shrinking one means it's losing ground, even as the tournament itself has grown from 13 teams to 32 (and 48 from 2026).

Viz 2 — The knockout-stage glass ceiling

Furthest stage reached by any team from each confederation, at every World Cup. Darker means a deeper run. Hover a cell to see which team actually got there — this is the clearest test of whether Africa and Asia are closing the gap on Europe and South America, or whether a structural ceiling still holds.

Viz 3 — Who actually wins it

Share of all 22 Men's World Cup titles won by each confederation. Slices ordered largest to smallest, starting at 12 o'clock. This is the sharpest version of the glass-ceiling story: reaching a semi-final is rare enough for Africa and Asia — actually winning has never happened for either, or for CONCACAF or Oceania. Click the UEFA or CONMEBOL slice to see which countries within it actually hold the titles.

Viz 4 — Does hosting actually help?

For every host nation, the furthest stage they reached while hosting (gold dot) against the average furthest stage that same country reached across all their other World Cup appearances (gray dot). A gold dot sitting well to the right of its gray partner means the home crowd corresponds with a real boost in performance.

Qatar 2022 shows a single dot — it was Qatar's first-ever World Cup appearance, so there's no away-tournament history to compare against. South Africa 2010 had only 2 prior appearances, both group-stage exits, so its "average" is a thin sample.

Confederation and furthest-stage data both derive from FIFA's own qualified-teams records (the Fjelstul World Cup database). "Furthest stage" is ranked group stage < 2nd group stage < round of 16 < quarter-final < semi-final < final (runner-up) < champion. Filtered to Men's World Cups only — the same source also contains Women's World Cup data (1991–2019), not included here.

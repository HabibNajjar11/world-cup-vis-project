Zooming into the matches themselves: has the football gotten more cautious, when do goals actually happen, has fear of losing changed how knockout games end, and what really happens to the team that won it all last time.

Viz 5 — Is the World Cup becoming more defensive?

Average goals scored per match, every World Cup since 1930. The explosive mid-century era gives way to a much flatter, lower-scoring modern game.

Viz 6 — The psychology of the 90th minute

All 2,720 World Cup goals, binned by when in the match they happened. The 45+ and 90+ bars isolate goals scored specifically in stoppage time — the moment fatigue, desperation, and a ticking clock collide.

ET = extra time (all periods combined). Own goals are included, since they're still goals shaped by the same late-match pressure. Bins aren't all equal width in real minutes — the stoppage-time bins (45+, 90+) are typically only a few minutes long, and "ET" spans up to 30 — so treat bar height here as a goal count per labeled interval, not a strict density.

Viz 7 — The cruel tiebreaker

Share of knockout-stage matches (not all matches — group games can't go to extra time) that were forced into extra time or a penalty shootout, by decade.

Viz 8 — The champion's curse

Where the reigning champion finished at the very next World Cup. A dip toward the bottom means an early, shocking exit — watch the 2010→2018 stretch, where three straight defending champions (Italy, Spain, Germany) were knocked out in the group stage.

Uruguay's 1930 title has no bar — reigning champions, they didn't even enter the 1934 tournament, in part retaliating against the European teams who'd snubbed the inaugural World Cup.

Goals and match data derive from the Fjelstul World Cup database, filtered to Men's World Cups only. "Furthest stage" scale: group stage (1) through final (6) / champion (7).

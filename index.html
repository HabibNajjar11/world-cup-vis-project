World Cup: geopolitics and the balance of power

Three dashboards, one question — has the World Cup actually globalized, or just grown?

The story connecting all three parts

On the surface, the World Cup looks like it has democratized. It has grown from 13 teams in 1930 to 32 (and soon 48), pulling in confederations that barely had a seat at the table in the early decades. Crowds have only gotten bigger. More countries than ever get a shot at the world's biggest stage.

Part 1 tests whether that growth is real or just cosmetic. It turns out the tournament has expanded far faster than power has actually redistributed: only two confederations have ever won it, and reaching even a semi-final remains a near-impossible ceiling for the rest of the world. Hosting, meanwhile, still delivers a real and measurable edge — the expansion is genuine, but the ceiling above most of the world hasn't moved.

Part 2 zooms into the pitch itself, and finds that even for the nations that do have power, nothing is secure. The game has gotten more cautious and lower-scoring, penalty shootouts — nonexistent before 1982 — are now a normal part of a modern World Cup, and reigning champions are increasingly likely to collapse in the group stage the very next time out. Structural advantage at the top doesn't buy safety match to match.

Part 3 looks at the human and cultural layer around all of it — the fans, the discipline, the people trusted to actually run a winning team — and finds the same tension again in a different form. Crowds keep growing and discipline has its own history of specific flashpoints, but when it comes to who gets picked to manage a champion, an old pattern still holds: a manager from the country he coaches has historically outperformed an imported foreign name, and for those local managers, experience keeps compounding in a way it doesn't for outsiders.

Put together, the three dashboards trace the same fault line from three different angles: the World Cup has globalized in scale, but not in outcome. Part 1 shows the structure of that gap, Part 2 shows how fragile even the powerful are within it, and Part 3 shows how deeply the old patterns are woven into the culture around the game.
